MAP 12.1
Distribution of the Slave Population in 1790, 1830, and 1860

The cotton boom shifted the African American population to the South and West. In 1790, most
slaves lived and worked on Chesapeake tobacco and Carolina rice and indigo plantations. By 1830,
those areas were still heavily populated by black families, but hundreds of thousands of slaves also
labored on the cotton and sugar lands of the Lower Mississippi Valley and on cotton plantations
in Georgia and northern Florida. Three decades later, the majority of blacks lived and worked
along the Mississippi River and in an arc of fertile cotton lands — the “black belt” — sweeping from
Mississippi through South Carolina.
